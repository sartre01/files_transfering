Health Inequities and Disparities in the Latinx Community: A Closer Look at Type 2 Diabetes

Chris Pedretti

Population Background

The Hispanic/Latinx population in the United States is a diverse group with roots in various countries across Latin America, including Mexico, Puerto Rico, Cuba, and Central and South America. This population constitutes one of the largest and fastest-growing ethnic groups in the country. As of recent data, Hispanics/Latinx individuals make up over “19.1% of the total U.S. population”, with projections indicating continued growth. Despite the cultural richness and diversity within this population, Hispanic/Latinx individuals often face significant health disparities and inequities compared to non-Hispanic white counterparts. These disparities manifest in various health outcomes, with diabetes being a notable example.

Health Disparity Contextualized

Prevalence: Diabetes is a chronic metabolic condition characterized by elevated blood sugar levels. Among Hispanic/Latinx individuals in the United States, the prevalence of diabetes is notably higher compared to non-Hispanic white individuals. This health disparity is evident across all age groups within the Hispanic/Latinx community.
Risk Factors: Multiple factors contribute to the increased risk of diabetes among Hispanic/Latinx individuals. These include genetic predisposition, socioeconomic factors, lifestyle behaviors, cultural factors, and limited access to healthcare services. For example, Hispanics/Latinx individuals often have higher rates of obesity, which is a significant risk factor for type 2 diabetes.
Complications: Diabetes can lead to severe complications such as cardiovascular disease, kidney disease, nerve damage, and vision impairment if not effectively managed. Studies have shown that Hispanic/Latinx individuals with diabetes are at a higher risk of experiencing these complications compared to other racial/ethnic groups.

Taking A Look At The Data

National Data: According to the US Department of Health and Human Services Office of Minority Health, Hispanic/Latinx adults are “70% more likely to be diagnosed with diabetes than non-Hispanic white adults”. The American Diabetes Association reports that about “12.5% of Hispanic/Latinx adults in the U.S. have diabetes, compared to 7.5% of non-Hispanic white adults”.
State Data: In California, the Hispanic/Latinx community has higher rates of diabetes compared to other racial/ethnic groups. According to the California Health Interview Survey (CHIS), the prevalence of diagnosed diabetes among Hispanic/Latinx adults in California is approximately 12.5%, while it is 7.5% among non-Hispanic white adults.
Local Data: Los Angeles County Department of Public Health data indicates that among Hispanic/Latinx adults, the age-adjusted prevalence of diagnosed diabetes is around 11.8%, higher than the county average of 9.6%. Furthermore, Hispanic/Latinx individuals are more likely to experience diabetes-related complications such as lower extremity amputations and end-stage renal disease.

Social Determinants of Health Contributing to Diabetes

Socioeconomic Status: Many Hispanic/Latinx individuals face socioeconomic challenges such as limited access to quality healthcare, lower levels of education, and income disparities, which increase their risk of diabetes.
Access to Proper Nutrition/Location: Factors such as food insecurity, limited access to nutritious foods, and neighborhood environments lacking safe spaces for physical activity contribute to higher diabetes rates among Hispanic/Latinx communities. This is tied to food deserts and the presence of grocery stores in low income neighborhoods.
Healthcare Access and Utilization: Limited access to healthcare services, language barriers, and lack of health insurance coverage prevent many Hispanic/Latinx individuals from receiving regular diabetes screenings, education, and management.
Cultural Factors/Generational Trends: Cultural norms around food, family roles, and health-seeking behaviors can influence diabetes risk and management within the Hispanic/Latinx community.
Education: Education impacts an individual's “health literacy” in that those who have the knowledge of how to seek proper care and take better care of their health.

Potential Future Health Programs

Community Health Education and Outreach Programs: Develop culturally tailored health education programs focused on diabetes prevention, management, and lifestyle modifications. These programs should be delivered in Spanish and take into account cultural beliefs, traditions, and family dynamics. Community health workers or promotores de salud can play a vital role in delivering these programs within Hispanic/Latinx neighborhoods.
Telehealth Services: Expand access to telehealth services, including virtual consultations with healthcare providers, diabetes management programs, and educational resources. Telehealth can overcome barriers related to transportation, language, and geographic location, making healthcare more accessible for Hispanic/Latinx individuals, especially those living in rural or underserved areas.
Policy Advocacy and System-Level Changes: Advocate for policy changes and system-level interventions to address underlying social determinants of health contributing to the diabetes disparity among Hispanic/Latinx communities. This may include efforts to improve access to affordable healthcare, expand health insurance coverage, increase funding for community health programs, and promote healthy food environments in neighborhoods.
